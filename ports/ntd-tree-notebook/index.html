I. Tree Species and Their Characteristics

This section details various tree species, highlighting their identifying features, growth habits, and notable characteristics.

American Holly (Ilex opaca)

  • Identified by its distinctive green, spiny leaves and the presence of new, lighter green growth near the base of the trunk. (Source: "American Holly_240315_155421.pdf")
  • A full tree shows a conical shape with dense foliage. (Source: "American Holly_240315_155421.pdf")

Ash (Fraxinus spp.)

  • Three species are discussed: white ash (F. americana), green ash (F. pennsylvanica), and blue ash (F. quadrangulata). While distinct in the tree form, their woods are generally similar. (Source: "Ash _231005_141215 (1).pdf")
  • Wood Characteristics: Heartwood is generally brown, sapwood is light yellow to cream-colored. Second growth trees have a large proportion of sapwood. Blue ash has a "fish scale" pattern in its bark. Ash wood is heavy, strong, hard, stiff, and has good resistance to shock. It retains its shape well and resists warping and shrinking. The grain is distinct but the wood is porous. It is used for tool handles, oars, furniture, and interior trim. (Source: "Ash _231005_141215 (1).pdf")
  • Emerald Ash Borer (EAB) Detection: EAB is a small, metallic green beetle, 1/2 inch long, and attacks only ash trees. Signs of infestation include winding S-shaped tunnels just under the bark, small D-shaped holes on the trunk (1/8 inch wide), sparse leaves, new sprouts from the trunk/lower branches, and increased activity by woodpeckers. (Source: "Ash _231005_141215 (1).pdf")

Bald Cypress (Taxodium distichum)

  • Commonly called swamp cypress, red cypress, white cypress, or yellow cypress. It primarily grows in the southern United States on river bottoms or wet sites, extending up the Mississippi River into the Bootheel of Missouri. (Source: "Bald Cypress_231005_141236 (1).pdf")
  • Wood Characteristics: The heartwood is dark reddish brown, characterized by loose, shreddy ridges, and is noted for durability against rot and decay. The heartwood color varies widely from light yellowish brown to dark brownish red or chocolate brown. Sapwood is narrow, nearly white, and less durable. The wood is light, strong, hard, and stiff, with minimal shrinkage. It has a slightly oily feel and a rancid odor. (Source: "Bald Cypress_231005_141236 (1).pdf")
  • Uses: Cypress wood is easily used by planing mills for sash, doors, exterior trim, siding, posts, beams, and various other lumber products. It's suitable for railroad ties, car construction, poles, piling, shingles, slack cooperage, and fence posts. (Source: "Bald Cypress_231005_141236 (1).pdf")
  • Growth Conditions: Does well in areas with low soil aeration, such as flooded and urban areas. While it can tolerate water, it does not prefer it but thrives in suitable conditions without it. (Source: "Bald Cypress_231005_141236 (1)_240207_143942.pdf", "Bald Cypress_241019_155805.pdf")

Bigtooth Aspen (Populus grandidentata)

  • Photos show tall, slender trees with light-colored bark, common in dense stands. (Source: "Bigtooth Aspen_240402_145707.pdf")
  • Pictures were taken at Sand Pond Conservation Area, MO. (Source: "Bigtooth Aspen_240402_145707.pdf")

Black Cherry (Prunus serotina)

  • Identified by its distinctive bark, which appears somewhat flaky or peeling in segments, and its dark, ripe fruit. (Source: "Black Cherry_240703_102512.pdf")
  • Fruit ripens around July 2nd and has a bitter taste. (Source: "Black Cherry_240703_102512.pdf")

Black Gum (Nyssa sylvatica)

  • Native to the Ozarks and Bootheel area of Missouri, also known as tupelo, sourgum, or pepperidge. (Source: "Black Gum_231005_141158 (1).pdf")
  • Identification: Bark can resemble elm, but watch for horizontal branches which are characteristic. (Source: "Black Gum_231005_141158 (1).pdf")
  • Wood Characteristics: Sapwood is yellowish to brownish white; heartwood is reddish brown, almost black, and irregularly shaped. The grain is interlocked, and the wood is diffuse porous, making growth rings difficult to discern. It is fine and uniform, difficult to dry, but very strong. The wood is heavy, tough, and durable. (Source: "Black Gum_231005_141158 (1).pdf")
  • Uses: Used for pallets and boxes, moldings, furniture parts, and railroad ties. Pulpwood is a major use. (Source: "Black Gum_231005_141158 (1).pdf")
  • Growth: Grows well on upland and bottomland sites, reaching better size on deeper or richer soils. Leaves turn a deep, brilliant red in fall. (Source: "Black Gum_231005_141158 (1).pdf")

Black Walnut (Juglans nigra)

  • Grows up to 90 feet tall with a rounded, open crown. (Source: "Black Walnut_231005_141141 (1).pdf")
  • Bark: Rough, and when the surface is lightly scraped, it shows a chocolate brown color. (Source: "Black Walnut_231005_141141 (1).pdf")
  • Leaves: Have 13-23 leaflets, pointed with toothed edges. (Source: "Black Walnut_231005_141141 (1).pdf")
  • Nuts: Green husks turn black as they ripen. (Source: "Black Walnut_231005_141141 (1).pdf")
  • Wood Characteristics: Sapwood is creamy white to pale brown, heartwood is light gray brown to dark purplish brown. The wood is hard, strong, heavy, and valuable for furniture, gunstocks, picture frames, and other products. High-grade walnut veneer is exported. (Source: "Black Walnut_231005_141141 (1).pdf")
  • Culinary Uses: Nuts are collected for snacks, candies, and ice cream; shells are used as filler and polishing agent. (Source: "Black Walnut_231005_141141 (1).pdf")
  • Thousand Cankers Disease (TCD): This disease is fatal to black walnut trees, typically killing trees in 2-3 years. It is caused by a fungus carried by the walnut twig beetle. Signs include black cankers (dime-sized) with beetle galleries, beetle entry holes (Walnut Twig Beetle), dead leaves still attached, yellowing/thinning of the upper crown, and dark amber stain on still-attached bark. Immediate tree removal is recommended. (Source: "Black Walnut_231005_141141 (1).pdf")

Blackjack Oak (Quercus marilandica)

  • Identified by its deeply furrowed, blocky bark, which is grayish-brown. (Source: "Blackjack Oak_231101_140533 (1).pdf", "Blackjack Oak_231101_140533 (1)_240404_060505.pdf")
  • Pictures were taken in Sunklands 7. (Source: "Blackjack Oak_231101_140533 (1)_240404_060505.pdf")

Butternut (Juglans cinerea)

  • Characterized by its distinctively ridged and furrowed bark. (Source: "Butternut_240313_073618.pdf")

Cherrybark Oak (Quercus pagoda)

  • Has dark, rough bark with deep furrows. Leaves are deeply lobed. (Source: "Cherrybark Oak_240404_060625.pdf")
  • Pictures were taken at Sand Pond CA. (Source: "Cherrybark Oak_240404_060625.pdf")

Eastern White Pine (Pinus strobus)

  • A tall conifer, often with reddish-brown upper foliage. (Source: "E White Pine_230923_162551 (1).pdf")

Flowering Dogwood (Cornus florida)

  • Blooms in late early spring, after the redbud. (Source: "Flowering Dogwood_240411_140008.pdf")
  • Features an "alligator-like" block bark pattern. (Source: "Flowering Dogwood_240411_140008.pdf")
  • An understory tree, less frequent on edges compared to redbud. (Source: "Flowering Dogwood_240411_140008.pdf")

Gum Bumelia (Sideroxylon lanuginosum)

  • A branch with thorns and small, newly emerging leaves. (Source: "Gum Bumelia_240406_105639.pdf")

Hazelnut (Corylus americana)

  • A slender, branching shrub, with catkins present in early spring. (Source: "Hazelnut_240313_151355.pdf")

Overcup Oak (Quercus lyrata)

  • Observed growing in standing water, indicating a preference for wet environments. (Source: "Overcup Oak_240402_140406.pdf")

Ozark Chinquapin (Castanea ozarkensis)

  • Growth: Produces in 3-5 years. (Source: "Ozark Chinquapin_241019_153953.pdf")
  • Ecological Importance: Attracts high amounts of insects for pollen and nectar, which in turn leads to higher bird species, especially whippoorwills. Research also indicates high moth visitation. (Source: "Ozark Chinquapin_241019_153953.pdf")
  • Light Tolerance: Shade intolerant. (Source: "Ozark Chinquapin_241019_153953.pdf")
  • Flowering: Trees bloom mid-May to early June. (Source: "Ozark Chinquapin_241019_153953.pdf")
  • Soil Preference: Does not like roots to be wet for long. Bracken Fern grows in similar places. (Source: "Ozark Chinquapin_241019_153953.pdf")
  • Root System: Features multiple tap roots spreading out laterally, with one initial tap root descending quickly for deep water. Has thick lateral roots and entangled mass of highly active fine hairy roots. Lateral roots extend further than the drip line. (Source: "Ozark Chinquapin_241019_153953.pdf")
  • Nutrition: Nuts are high in protein and carbohydrates, with Ozark Chinquapin showing higher protein (15.2%) and fat (13.93%) compared to White Oak and American/Chinese Chestnut. (Source: "Ozark Chinquapin_241019_153953.pdf")
  • Planting Tips:
    • Water well in the first year.
    • Requires well-drained soil.
    • Mulch trees (straw also works, not just wood chips).
    • Use natural bug spray.
    • Tubes are almost always required.
    • Plant on N/E slope if possible.
    • Some shade in the first year can be beneficial.
    • Recommended spacing is 25 feet.
    • Plant towards the top of a slope.
    • Even a small amount of water helps during drought years for establishment.
    • Cayenne pepper on leaves can deter deer. (Source: "Ozark Chinquapin_241019_153953.pdf")

Pecan (Carya illinoinensis)

  • Treated separately from other hickories, though often mixed with them in the broad, flat river bottoms of the Mississippi and Missouri rivers. (Source: "Pecan_231005_141258 (1).pdf")
  • Growth: Found primarily on deep, moist soils, but also on upland sites. (Source: "Pecan_231005_141258 (1).pdf")
  • Fruit: A favorite nut tree known for its sweet nutmeat. (Source: "Pecan_231005_141258 (1).pdf")
  • Wood Characteristics: Sapwood is creamy white to pale brown, heartwood is pale brown to reddish brown, sometimes with streaks of a darker hue. Pecan wood is heavy, very strong, hard, and stiff. It machines well and finishes well, but shrinks significantly when dried. It is durable, but susceptible to insect attacks. (Source: "Pecan_231005_141258 (1).pdf")
  • Uses: Major use is in furniture and wall paneling. Can be used for tool handles (especially impact tools), ladder rungs, flooring, and special products requiring a strong, tough, elastic wood. Used in smoking meats and as fuel wood. (Source: "Pecan_231005_141258 (1).pdf")
  • Landscaping: Makes a beautiful shade tree and is commonly planted. (Source: "Pecan_231005_141258 (1).pdf")

Persimmon (Diospyros virginiana)

  • Tall trees, often found in wetlands. (Source: "Persimmon_240404_060541.pdf")
  • Pictures were taken at Sand Pond CA. (Source: "Persimmon_240404_060541.pdf")

Pignut Hickory (Carya glabra)

  • Identified by its rough, furrowed bark and distinctive terminal bud. Leaves turn brown in the fall. (Source: "Pignut Hickory _231101_141007 (1).pdf", "Pignut Hickory _231101_141007 (1)_240606_130007.pdf")

Red Cedar (Juniperus virginiana)

  • Also known as eastern red cedar or aromatic red cedar, this is a juniper that grows throughout Missouri, especially in the southwestern Ozarks. (Source: "Red Cedar_231005_141416 (1).pdf")
  • Growth: An invader of old fields and open areas, with birds dispersing seeds. Grows rapidly, displacing native flora. (Source: "Red Cedar_231005_141416 (1).pdf")
  • Wood Characteristics: Two distinct features are its odor and its light to dark or pinkish-red heartwood. Slow-growing trees yield high percentages of red heartwood, while fast-growing trees have mostly sapwood (creamy white). The heartwood is very resistant to decay. (Source: "Red Cedar_231005_141416 (1).pdf")
  • Uses: Used for closet lining, pencils, fence posts. Shavings are popular for pet litter due to their odor. (Source: "Red Cedar_231005_141416 (1).pdf")
  • Fire Ecology: "Contains volatile oils, low branches make it a ladder fuel, burns hot and flings ash with high flames." Fire suppression favors Red Cedar by eliminating competing grasses. Overgrazing also contributes to its spread by reducing fire load and opening bare soil for sapling establishment. (Source: "Red Cedar_231005_141416 (1).pdf")
  • Management: Control methods include burning and hand chopping (cutting below the crown kills the tree). Experiments are underway to find a seedless windbreak cultivar. Heavy overstory canopy still allows for growth in late fall/winter due to soil alteration/pH. Trimming the leader can force width expansion. (Source: "Red Cedar_231005_141416 (1).pdf")

Scarlett Oak (Quercus coccinea)

  • Distinguished by its brilliant red fall foliage, which turns color later than other trees. (Source: "Scarlett Oak_231101_140624 (1).pdf", "Scarlett Oak_231101_140624 (1)_240126_080050.pdf")

Serviceberry (Amelanchier spp.)

  • Characterized by delicate white blossoms appearing early in spring before leaves fully emerge. It has light-colored, smooth bark with some darker patches. (Source: "Service berry_240313_073344.pdf")

Shortleaf Pine (Pinus echinata)

  • The only pine species native to Missouri and a principal southern yellow pine. Found on dry, sandy soils, often in pure stands or mixed with oaks and hickories. (Source: "Shortleaf Pine _231213_140834 (1).pdf")
  • Bark: Irregular plates of cinnamon red color. (Source: "Shortleaf Pine _231213_140834 (1).pdf")
  • Wood Characteristics: Sapwood is yellowish to white and narrow; heartwood is reddish brown. Short-leaf pine is moderately heavy, strong, and stable when properly seasoned. Used as lumber, sheathing, flooring, boxes, millwork, pallets, and railroad ties. "Shortleaf creates denser, stronger growth rings compared to other southern yellow Pines and thus results in greater quality lumber." (Source: "Shortleaf Pine _231213_140834 (1).pdf", "Shortleaf Pine _231213_140834 (1)_240208_085701.pdf")
  • Regeneration: Historically the most dominant Southern Yellow Pine in the USA. Possesses a "basal crook" J-root where it sprouts from fire; roots continue to grow after top-kill and "eventually explode in growth". Containerized seedlings have higher survival. (Source: "Shortleaf Pine _231213_140834 (1).pdf")
  • Decline Factors: Fire suppression and fence laws. Intensive harvests historically removed overstory but didn't shade out understory. (Source: "Shortleaf Pine _231213_140834 (1).pdf")
  • Optimal Growth: Growth is optimized at lower stocking (less than 40%). Most competitive at lower stocking levels. (Source: "Shortleaf Pine _231213_140834 (1)_240208_085701.pdf")
  • Seeding: "The first year of a shortleaf seeding is the least impacted by overstory stocking." Years 2-9 are critical for establishing height advantage and ensuring survival and quality. (Source: "Shortleaf Pine _231213_140834 (1)_240208_085701.pdf")
  • Fire and Regeneration: "Disturbance right before seedfall is the best natural regen strategy. Burning is an excellent tool for this, but is generally not an option without the manpower, unless an overwhelming need dictates." (Source: "Shortleaf Pine _231213_140834 (1).pdf")

Sweetgum (Liquidambar styraciflua)

  • Tall, straight trunk, often found in dense forest settings. Can have "wings" or corky ridges on its branches. (Source: "Sweetgum_240402_145733.pdf")

White Oak (Quercus alba)

  • Susceptible to Rapid White Oak Mortality (RWOM). This affects trees on lower slopes or drainages, particularly sites favorable for white oak. Age is not a factor, but trees with diameters of 10-18 inches are affected. A non-native root pathogen, Phytophthora cinnamomi, is common on these sites. (Source: "White Oak_231017_103548 (1).pdf")

Willow Oak (Quercus phellos)

  • A tall tree with a straight trunk and generally smooth bark. (Source: "Willow Oak_240404_060518.pdf")
  • Pictures were taken at Sand Pond CA. (Source: "Willow Oak_240404_060518.pdf")

Black Willow (Salix nigra)

  • Known as "Salix" and swamp willow or willow. It is the largest and most widespread native willow in Missouri. (Source: "Willow_231005_141348 (1).pdf")
  • Habitat: Reaches its best size on rich bottomland soils and is found throughout the state around almost any water source. It frequently invades newly deposited, bare soils in river or creek bottoms. (Source: "Willow_231005_141348 (1).pdf")
  • Wood Characteristics: Sapwood is light gray to tan, in some cases nearly white. Heartwood varies from light gray to dark reddish brown. Bark is dark brown or blackish with deep furrows. Texture is fine and uniform, easy to work. It tends to warp and crook unless restrained. Generally considered not durable when exposed to conditions favoring decay. (Source: "Willow_231005_141348 (1).pdf")
  • Uses: Primarily used for lumber, often somewhat weak, but used in boxes, crates, beehives, and toys. (Source: "Willow_231005_141348 (1).pdf")
  • Stream Bank Stabilization: Willow cuttings can be used for stream bank stabilization. This involves a fabric/tarp layer, mulch/hay layer, and 3-foot cut willow sticks inserted, which prevents the need for draining later. Utilize overgrown willow in an area, as it will grow back from a small stump. (Source: "Willow_231005_141348 (1).pdf")

II. Plant Management and Issues

This section covers general tree care, disease, and problematic plant species.

Apple Tree Pruning and Care

  • Growth Principle: "More growth = more leaves." (Source: "Apple Trees_230922_100541 (1).pdf")
  • Pruning Effects: Does not alter natural growth height/width. Severe pruning causes more epicormics, less colorful fruit, and longer maturation time. (Source: "Apple Trees_230922_100541 (1).pdf")
  • Cutting Techniques: Cut on the outer collar at a downward angle. Avoid narrow crotches/rollers as they create weak branches prone to splitting under fruit/snow load. (Source: "Apple Trees_230922_100541 (1).pdf")
  • Timing: Prune February-April (dormant season) to minimize damage, avoiding early winter/fall. (Source: "Apple Trees_230922_100541 (1).pdf")
  • Specific Pruning: Prune broken/dead branches. Cut top leader to a well-developed horizontal branch to keep apples/branches within reach. Remove vertical branches that rub others. More heavy pruning near the top allows light to the bottom. (Source: "Apple Trees_230922_100541 (1).pdf")
  • Epicormics: Remove epicormics around August 1st. (Source: "Apple Trees_230922_100541 (1).pdf")
  • Soil PH: Ideal soil pH is 6.2-6.5. (Source: "Apple Trees_230922_100541 (1).pdf")
  • Fertilization: Apply 1 lb. nitrogen fertilizer if growing <8 inches a year; otherwise, 1 lb. potash/magnesium for every 5 apples. (Source: "Apple Trees_230922_100541 (1).pdf")
  • Fruit Issues: If fruit has brown corky eraser-sized areas, water well in the dry season and apply 1 cup of Borax evenly every 3-7 years (for Boron deficiency). (Source: "Apple Trees_230922_100541 (1).pdf")
  • Vole Damage: If trees are girdled by voles, use plastic/wire mouse guards (remove in spring) and keep tall grasses away/mowed. (Source: "Apple Trees_230922_100541 (1).pdf")
  • Fruit Thinning: Do not allow double/triple fruit clusters; pick apples spaced 6-8 inches after Natural June Drop, before July. (Source: "Apple Trees_230922_100541 (1).pdf")
  • Insect Issues: Require careful consideration. Apple-Magot traps are available. Apple-scab resistant varieties exist; otherwise, destroy fallen leaves. (Source: "Apple Trees_230922_100541 (1).pdf")
  • Diseased Tips: Ruined tips can be cut 6-12 inches away; disinfect tools. (Source: "Apple Trees_230922_100541 (1).pdf")

Conifer Trainer Trees for Black Walnut

  • Purpose: To produce clear, veneer-quality black walnut stems by shading out lower walnut branches while leaving clear sky overhead. Black walnut's natural shade is not dense enough for this. (Source: "Conifer Trainer Trees_240927_112944.pdf")
  • Trainer Species: White pine and bald cypress have been successfully used. Dawn redwood also showed good growth. Scots pine was rejected due to Juglone resistance, and Eastern hemlock, Douglas fir, and European larch failed in trials. (Source: "Conifer Trainer Trees_240927_112944.pdf")
  • Delayed Start: A 5-year head start for the white pine trainers on the walnut is recommended. Accidental results showed "beautiful pencil straight walnuts 15 years later" when this delay was roughly followed. (Source: "Conifer Trainer Trees_240927_112944.pdf")
  • Spacing: Young white pines were on 8' x 8' centers. (Source: "Conifer Trainer Trees_240927_112944.pdf")
  • Soil pH Importance: White pine thrives on well-drained, sandy, moderately acid soils (pH = 5.5). It usually "will not survive" in poorly drained, compacted soil with high clay content or a pH above 6.5. This was identified as the reason for complete white pine failure at Blennerhassett Island (pH 6.9-7.2). (Source: "Conifer Trainer Trees_240927_112944.pdf")
  • Candidate Criteria for Trainers:
    1. Offered by state tree nurseries (affordability).
    2. Compatible with the black walnut site's soil and hardiness zone.
    3. Growth rate matches or slightly exceeds black walnut's.
    4. Somewhat resistant to soil juglone.
    5. (Optional) Local timber market for trainer species at thinning time. (Source: "Conifer Trainer Trees_240927_112944.pdf")
  • Soil pH Ranges for Conifers (Table 1):
    • Baldcypress: 4.6-7.5
    • Pine, Shortleaf: 4.5-7.0
    • Pine, E. White: >4.0 (best at 5.5, fails above 6.5)
    • Pine, Scots: 4.5-6.0
    • Pine, Loblolly: 4.5-7.0
    • Pine, Slash: 4.5-6.5
    (Source: "Conifer Trainer Trees_240927_112944.pdf")
